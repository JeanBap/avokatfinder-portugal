Licença de Utilização

Portuguese: Licença de Utilização (Habitation Licence)

Portuguese habitation licence certifying that a building is legally authorised for its intended use — typically residential. Required for all buildings constructed after 1951 and essential for property sales.

TL;DRPortuguese habitation licence certifying that a building is legally authorised for its intended use — typically residential. Required for all buildings constructed after 1951 and essential for property sales.

The licença de utilização (also called licença de habitabilidade for residential properties) is the Portuguese municipal certificate confirming that a building has been constructed in accordance with its approved plans and is legally authorised for its intended use. It is issued by the local Câmara Municipal (municipality) after inspection and is required for all buildings constructed after 7 August 1951.

A valid licença de utilização is essential for any Portuguese property sale post-1951. The notário is required to verify its existence before proceeding with the escritura pública. Without a valid licence, the sale cannot legally proceed — or if it does, the buyer acquires a property with an unresolved legal status that will affect future sales, financing, and potentially the ability to register for utilities.

Pre-1951 properties are not required to have a licença de utilização — they are presumed lawfully constructed. This is why many old Lisbon and Porto buildings and virtually all rural properties built before this date are sold without one. However, any subsequent construction or conversion works on a pre-1951 property may require their own licença.

Ruined properties (ruínas), agricultural buildings converted to residential use without permission, and self-built properties without approved plans often lack a licença. Regularising the absence of a licença (through a legalisation process — licenciamento extraordinário) can be complex, expensive, and in some cases impossible where the property was built in an area not zoned for residential use.

For expat buyers, checking the licença de utilização status is critical due diligence — particularly for rural properties, barn conversions, and older urban apartments where the current use may differ from the original approved use. Request a copy of the licença from the seller and verify it with the local Câmara Municipal before signing the CPCV.

⚠ Common MistakePurchasing a converted rural property (e.g. barn, agricultural building) without verifying that the conversion has a licença de utilização for residential use. Agricultural buildings converted without planning permission lack legal residential status — potentially making them unsaleable to future buyers and unfinanceable with a standard Portuguese mortgage.
💡 Expert TipRequest a certidão de teor from the Câmara Municipal for the property you are buying. This document shows the planning history, including any approved projects and licences issued. A €20–50 administrative fee gives you the complete official picture of what has been lawfully approved — far more reliable than relying on the seller's representations.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if the property I want to buy doesn't have a licença de utilização?

Options include: (1) requiring the seller to regularise it before completion; (2) reducing your offer price to reflect the cost and risk of regularisation; or (3) walking away. Never complete a purchase of a post-1951 property without a licença or a clear regularisation plan agreed in writing with the seller.

Does a licença de utilização guarantee the property is in good structural condition?

No — the licença confirms the property was inspected and approved at the time of issue, not that it is currently in good condition. A separate structural survey (vistoria) by a licensed engineer is always advisable, particularly for older properties.

Is the licença de utilização the same as the licença de construção?

No — the licença de construção authorises construction to begin; the licença de utilização is issued after construction is complete and inspected, authorising the building to be occupied. A property may have a construction licence but no utilisation licence if it was never formally inspected at completion.
