Escritura Pública

Portuguese: Escritura Pública (Notarial Deed)

The final notarial deed that legally transfers ownership of Portuguese property. Signed before a notário, it is the definitive document recorded in the land registry (Conservatória do Registo Predial).

TL;DRThe final notarial deed that legally transfers ownership of Portuguese property. Signed before a notário, it is the definitive document recorded in the land registry (Conservatória do Registo Predial).

The escritura pública is the culmination of any Portuguese property transaction — the formal notarial deed signed before a notário (notary) that definitively transfers legal ownership from seller to buyer. Unlike many European countries where a preliminary agreement creates a binding obligation, Portuguese law requires the escritura pública as the document that actually passes title.

Both buyer and seller (or their legal representatives via procuração — power of attorney) must attend the signing. The notário reads the full document aloud, verifies the identity of all parties, checks that all applicable taxes have been paid, and confirms the property's registration status. The escritura is then signed by all parties and the notário, who retains the original (minute) and issues authenticated copies (certidões) to each party.

Before the escritura can proceed, the buyer must present: proof of payment of IMT (Imposto Municipal sobre as Transmissões — property transfer tax) and Imposto de Selo (stamp duty), the caderneta predial (tax registration document from the Finanças), the certidão permanente (permanent land registry certificate), and in some cases planning certificates (licença de utilização). The notário checks all these documents before proceeding.

After signing, the notário submits the registration application to the Conservatória do Registo Predial (land registry). In Portugal, the registration is constitutive for mortgage purposes but declaratory for ownership — meaning the buyer legally owns the property from the moment of signing the escritura, but the registration is still essential for protection against third-party claims.

For expat buyers who cannot attend in person, a procuração (power of attorney) — authenticated and if signed abroad, apostilled — authorises a representative to sign on your behalf. Portuguese notários are accustomed to handling international procurações and can advise on the precise format required for your nationality and country of residence.

⚠ Common MistakePaying the purchase price directly to the seller at or before the escritura without using escrow. In Portugal, the standard practice is to pay by bank cheque (cheque bancário) at the escritura table — funds exchanged simultaneously with the signed deed. Advance payment without the deed signed leaves you vulnerable.
💡 Expert TipRequest a draft (minutia / projecto de escritura) from the notário at least one week before signing. Review it carefully with a bilingual Portuguese lawyer. Check that the property description, the parties' details, and all conditions from the CPCV (promissory contract) are accurately reflected.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use any notário in Portugal for my property purchase?

Yes — both buyer and seller are free to choose any Portuguese notário. The buyer typically pays the notário's fees as part of acquisition costs. In practice, many buyers use a notário recommended by their lawyer or the estate agent, but you are entitled to choose independently.

What if errors are discovered in the escritura after signing?

Minor errors can be corrected by a deed of rectification (escritura de rectificação) signed before the same or any notário. Substantive errors — wrong parties, wrong property — require more complex legal proceedings. Always review the draft carefully before signing.

Is the escritura pública different from a certidão?

Yes — the escritura is the original deed signed by all parties and the notário. A certidão is an authenticated copy of the escritura, issued by the notário's office. The certidão is what you keep and use; the original minute stays at the notary's archive.
