What is CPCV (Contrato de Promessa de Compra e Venda)?

Portugal's preliminary property purchase contract — the binding promise to buy and sell that precedes the final notarial deed (escritura).

Key Facts — CPCV (Contrato de Promessa de Compra e Venda)
  • Binding preliminary contract committing both parties to the sale
  • Deposit (sinal): typically 10–30% of purchase price
  • Buyer default: deposit forfeited; Seller default: double deposit returned
  • Include conditions precedent — consult your lawyer before signing
  • CPCV can be registered at Land Registry for extra buyer protection

The CPCV — Contrato de Promessa de Compra e Venda — is the preliminary purchase and sale agreement signed by buyer and seller in a Portuguese property transaction. It commits both parties to completing the final sale under the conditions set out in the contract and is typically accompanied by a deposit (sinal) paid by the buyer. The CPCV is the contract stage at which most of the negotiation of terms takes place and where your lawyer's involvement is most critical.

A well-drafted CPCV protects the buyer by including conditions precedent — specific conditions that must be met before the final deed (escritura) can proceed. Common conditions include: satisfactory results of a structural survey, confirmation of clear title at the Land Registry (Conservatória do Registo Predial), resolution of any outstanding mortgages or encumbrances, confirmation of planning compliance for the property's structures, and satisfactory energy performance certification. Your lawyer should draft or review the CPCV before you sign.

The deposit (sinal) paid at CPCV stage is typically 10–30% of the purchase price. If the buyer defaults (walks away without legal justification after the conditions precedent have been met), the deposit is forfeited to the seller. If the seller defaults (refuses to complete or sells to a third party), the seller must return double the deposit (arras em dobro) to the buyer under the default Portuguese Civil Code provisions. These rules can be modified by contract, making careful drafting essential.

The CPCV itself is not registered at the Land Registry in a standard transaction. However, if agreed by both parties, a CPCV can be registered (registo da promessa de compra e venda), which provides stronger protection for the buyer against subsequent sale to a third party or creditor claims against the property during the period between CPCV and escritura.

Timescales between CPCV and final escritura vary from a few weeks (for cash purchases with no complications) to several months (for transactions involving mortgage financing, planning compliance resolution, or complex title issues). Your lawyer manages this timeline and gives you clear notification when each stage is ready to proceed.

Common Mistake: Signing a CPCV drafted by the estate agent or seller's notário without having your own lawyer review it. The CPCV is the most important contract in a Portuguese property transaction and should never be signed without independent legal advice.
Expert Tip: Always ensure the CPCV specifies exactly which fixtures, fittings, and contents are included in the sale. In Portugal it is common for sellers to remove items that a buyer might assume are included (kitchen appliances, light fittings, built-in wardrobes). A specific inventory list attached to the CPCV prevents disputes at completion.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a lawyer for the CPCV stage?

Legally, no — but practically, yes. The CPCV is a binding contract with significant financial consequences if you default. Having a Portuguese lawyer review and negotiate the CPCV before you sign is one of the most important legal investments in the purchase process.

How long between CPCV and escritura?

Typically two to four months for straightforward cash purchases; longer if there is a mortgage (the bank's legal process adds time) or if any conditions precedent need resolution. The timeframe is agreed between parties and stated in the CPCV.

Can I pull out after signing a CPCV?

You can withdraw after signing a CPCV, but you will lose your deposit (sinal). Unless one of the agreed conditions precedent has not been met, there is no legal basis for a refund of the deposit if you simply change your mind after signing.
